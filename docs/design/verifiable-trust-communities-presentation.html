The mental model

Three layers, one trust fabric

Each layer was a separate design effort. Read top-to-bottom they are a single pipeline: a credential is held, presented into a governed decision, and the result becomes a community you live in.

Hold & PresentVTA · credential agent
A format-agnostic vault holds credentials encrypted at rest; the agent receives (OpenID4VCI), matches (DCQL), and presents (OpenID4VP) selectively, under per-disclosure consent. Trust the member carries.
DecideVTC · governed ceremonies
Every community state transition — join, leave, role-change, directory — is one policy pipeline producing a four-valued verdict, authored visually and dry-run in a live simulator before it ever touches state. Trust the community governs.
ExperienceOpenVTC · member CLI/TUI
A person bootstraps an account, mints per-community identities, joins many Verifiable Trust Communities, and runs concurrent live sessions from one Communities hub. Trust you actually live in.

The join ceremony is the spine that threads all three: OpenVTC submits a presentation → the VTA assembles it from held credentials → the VTC ceremony pipeline verifies and decides → the member is admitted and issued their membership credential.

Executive summary

Two shifts, one direction

Infrastructure shift

Authorization moved from “a row in an ACL + a role in a JWT” to “a verifiable credential the member holds and presents.”

Trust becomes portable, standards-native, privacy-preserving, and re-verifiable by anyone — not just by us.

Experience shift

Identity moved from “one persona, one authority, one flat context” to “an account with many personas across many communities.”

Belonging becomes plural: per-community identities, a Communities hub, and concurrent live sessions.

Portable

Membership and roles become W3C VCs the holder carries — usable beyond a single VTA/VTC, by external verifiers that show up later.

Governable

One decision pipeline per community, authored without code and simulated before activation. Policy is versioned, signed, and rollback-able.

Privacy-preserving

Selective disclosure (SD-JWT-VC, BBS+), per-disclosure consent records, and a vault with no enumeration primitive.

Before  →  After · architecture

What changed under the hood

Before — ACL & keys

  • VTA = key manager (BIP-32, signing oracle) + an ACL keyspace.
  • Authorization = an ACL entry + a role claim baked into a JWT.
  • Join = bespoke: submit a VP, an admin approves, the membership credential is handed back inline in the HTTP response.
  • Governance = accept-any default; raw-Rego upload; a flat status enum.
  • One persona, one mediator, one flat context — no notion of multi-membership.
⟶

After — hold, present & govern

  • VTA = credential agent with a format-agnostic vault (hold) + receive / match / present.
  • Authorization = a held Role VC → a verified-assertion cache; the ACL becomes a derived index.
  • Join = invite → hold → present (DCQL) → a governed ceremony verdict → admit + issue.
  • Governance = one default-deny pipeline per purpose; visual Rule IR → Rego; signed policy log + rollback.
  • Account → many personas → many communities, concurrent live sessions.
Layer 1 · Hold & Present

The member journey — invite → hold → present → join

A prospective member is invited, holds the invitation, presents it on demand, and is admitted — each arrow a standards-based protocol message.

1

Invite

The VTC issues a membership credential offer to the prospect's DID — an OpenID4VCI pre-authorized offer.

VTC → holder · offer
2

Hold

The VTA redeems with a key-binding proof, receives the credential, stores it in the vault (format-agnostic, encrypted).

holder ⇄ VTC · request → issue
3

Present

At join, the VTC sends a DCQL query. The VTA matches over its vault (no enumeration), gates on consent, builds a selectively-disclosed VP.

VTC → holder → VTC · query → present
4

Join

The VTC verifies the VP → runs the join ceremony → admits → issues VMC + Role VEC. The member now holds those too.

VTC · verify → decide → issue

Every step is the same credential machinery — issuance is presentation's mirror image. Build it once, reuse it for invites, memberships, roles, endorsements.

Layer 1 · Flow A · Issuance

OpenID4VCI over DIDComm — offer → request → issue

VTC (issuer)VTA (holder)Vault
① credential-exchange/offer — pre-authorized code (doubles as the proof nonce)
② credential-exchange/request — holder signs an OID4VCI key-binding proof (EdDSA, bound to its did:key)
③ Issuer verifies the proof → proven holder DID must equal the credential's subject (else Forbidden)
④ credential-exchange/issue — the credential (single-use offer consumed on success only)
⑤ Holder infers format, stores via the format-agnostic vault::receive (encrypted at rest)

Holder binding is the gate

A credential is released only to a party that proves possession of the subject key — the heart of OID4VCI, and the part every implementation otherwise hand-rolls.

Relayer ≠ holder is safe

DIDComm authcrypt authenticates the relayer; the inner key-binding proof authenticates the holder. They may differ (air-gapped onboarding) with no escalation.

Single-use, DoS-resistant

The pending offer is consumed only on a successful issue — a forged or wrong-party request can't burn the legitimate holder's offer.

Layer 1 · Flow B · Presentation

OpenID4VP + DCQL — query → match → present

VTC (verifier)VTA (holder)Vault
① credential-exchange/query — DCQL query + nonce + mandatory purpose (shown to the holder)
② Holder gathers candidates by the vct/type index — no wallet enumeration
③ DcqlQuery::match_credentials → which held credentials satisfy it + the claim paths to disclose
match
④ Consent gate (ISO/IEC 27560 + DPV) + selectively-disclosed present → vp_token
⑤ credential-exchange/present — holder-bound VP; verifier checks binding · status · issuer trust · DCQL

No-enumeration is a feature

The vault exposes no “list everything.” Discovery is only via an indexed field + value, so a query with no discriminator returns nothing — the holder never blind-scans its wallet to answer a verifier.

Disclose exactly what was consented

The reveal set is the consent record's personal-data list. SD-JWT-VC redacts to the consented subset; plain DI refuses to over-disclose.

Layer 2 · the working ceremony simulator

Dry-run any ceremony, live, before it touches state

Built into the VTC admin-UI and wired to the running daemon, the simulator assembles a verified-Facts document and evaluates the active policy via POST /v1/policies/{id}/test — the same decide() the routes run, minus the effect. State is never mutated.

Models the negotiation

A request_more verdict carries the needs it's missing. Satisfy a need and re-run — exactly the way an applicant would supply it and re-present. The threaded async loop, made tangible.

Authored without raw Rego

Operators edit a visual Rule IR that compiles to Rego + a Presentation Definition + English. The simulator checks invariants and shows the real four-valued verdict before activation.

Safe by construction

The test endpoint evaluates the active policy and returns a verdict — it never runs effects, so it can't issue, revoke, or admit. Inspect before you commit; version & roll back the policy if wrong.

  • One persona DID, one VTA, one mediator, one flat context.

  • A single DIDComm session / message loop.
  • No notion of communities, joining, or membership.
⟶

Regulatory alignment

Built to the EU's rulebook — eIDAS 2.0 & the EUDI Wallet

eIDAS 2.0 (Regulation (EU) 2024/1183) mandates EU Digital Identity Wallets, and the EUDI Architecture & Reference Framework (ARF) fixes the technical standards. We didn't choose our stack to match it — we chose open standards, and they are the ARF's stack. Alignment came for free.

Security model

Where the guarantees live

Holder key-binding proof

OID4VCI openid4vci-proof+jwt: verify typ/alg, resolve the kid did:key, check EdDSA (verify_strict), aud, freshness, and the c_nonce replay binding. Issuance releases only to the proven subject.

Consent-gated disclosure

A signed consent record (ISO/IEC 27560 + DPV) authorizes a verifier + claim set. The reveal set is the consent; revoked / temporally-invalid credentials are refused before any disclosure.

Crypto only in Verify

Policy reasons over a Verified* view; skipping verification doesn't compile. Default-deny posture; a signed, append-only policy log with fail-forward rollback and optional M-of-N governance.

No-enumeration vault + sealed transfer

No “list all” primitive — targeted index lookups only. Every secret-bearing bundle is HPKE-sealed to a client_did, armored, with a producer assertion + out-of-band SHA-256 digest.

Each was traced against forgery, replay (time + nonce), wrong-key, wrong-audience, alg-confusion, alg=none, and relayer-escalation attacks during review. Audience isolation separates VTA and VTC JWTs; cross-audience tokens are rejected.

Authentication · every pathway

Many ways to prove who you are — one auth surface

The /auth endpoints content-negotiate on the request shape: flat-JSON in → flat-JSON out, Trust-Task in → Trust-Task out. A new pathway is a new request shape, not a new endpoint — and freshness/replay is anchored by a single-use, TTL'd challenge throughout.

DI-signed Trust Task

A plain JSON auth/authenticate/0.1 whose holder eddsa-jcs-2022 proof is the authentication. No mediator — a REST-only VTA can authenticate. The whole login→refresh loop runs over plain HTTPS.

DIDComm v2 authcrypt

A packed message via the mediator; unpacking yields a cryptographically-proven sender DID. Works even when the VTA is private-network and reachable only through a mediator. Sender auth is intrinsic.

WebAuthn passkey

A passkey enrolled as an authentication verificationMethod in the published DID document. Any verifier resolves the DID and checks the assertion against the embedded key — the DID doc is the trust anchor.

Biometric step-up (AAL1→AAL2)

A sensitive operation escalates to AAL2 via a passkey/biometric approval — optionally delegated to a separate approver, who is push-woken on their device to consent.

Refresh carries no proof — the opaque rotated token is the bearer credential (OAuth2 §10.4). Together with the DI-signed path, mobile and browser clients run their full session lifecycle without ever needing a mediator, while DIDComm remains available for private-network and air-gapped peers.

The clients we built

A passkey in the browser, an agent in your pocket

Browser plugin — passkey ↔ DID bridge

  • Ships as a PWA and an MV3 extension (Vite + React) over a shared @openvtc/pnm-core.
  • Enrolls a WebAuthn passkey as a DID verificationMethod — prove control of a VTA-hosted DID from any site.
  • No DID private key ever leaves the VTA; no long-lived bearer token sits in browser storage.
  • Speaks REST and full DIDComm v2 (coordinate-mediation/2.0) — usable even when the VTA is mediator-only.

Mobile authenticator — iOS agent

  • A shared Rust engine, vta-mobile-core (UniFFI), under a native SwiftUI shell — one auth/credential brain, native skins.
  • Biometric AAL1→AAL2 step-up approver: Touch/Face ID approves the escalation.
  • Hardware key custody in the Secure Enclave (P-256); Ed25519/X25519 held in software (Tier 2).
  • Runs the entire build_authenticate → refresh loop over plain REST — no mediator required.

Both are holders in the Layer-1 sense — they hold keys/credentials, present proofs, and approve step-ups — over the very same open standards as the VTA and VTC.

The game changer

A programmable authentication & authorization layer

Authentication strength and authorization rules stopped being hard-coded. They are now runtime, governable configuration — proven by credentials, decided by policy you can simulate, escalated by risk.

Authentication — pluggable

One content-negotiated surface accepts DI-signed REST proofs, DIDComm authcrypt, WebAuthn passkeys, and delegated biometric step-up. Add a method by adding a request shape — clients, mediators, and verifiers are untouched.

Authorization — programmable

Not a hard-coded role check: a VC/VP the holder presents, evaluated by a Rego ceremony policy authored visually and dry-run in the simulator, with a configurable step-up policy (per-op-class AAL floor + per-entry override).

Why it changes the game: an operator changes who can do what, proven how, and how strongly by editing and simulating policy — not by shipping a release. Step-up becomes risk-based and per-operation; new auth methods and new authorization rules are additive; and every decision is the same verified-Facts → four-valued-verdict pipeline, auditable end-to-end.

ACT II

Why it matters, and what it unlocks

Act I was the architecture — three layers, built and largely live. Act II is the payoff: the benefits this foundation delivers today, and the capabilities it makes cheap to build next precisely because the hard parts are already done once.

The benefits

What this buys us — concretely

Portable trust

Membership and roles are credentials the member carries, not rows in our database. They survive beyond a single VTA/VTC and are re-verifiable by external parties that show up later.

Standards-native

Issuance and presentation ride OID4VCI / OID4VP + DCQL over DIDComm; policy compiles to Rego + Presentation Definitions. No bespoke signature schemes to defend — we delegate to well-tested libraries.

Privacy by construction

No persona at bootstrap, no wallet enumeration, selective disclosure, and per-disclosure consent. You reveal the minimum, where you choose, linkable only when you opt in.

Governance without engineers

Operators author ceremonies as visual rules, simulate the verdict live, version and roll back — no Rego, no redeploy, no waiting on a release. Policy becomes an operational lever.

Belonging is plural

One account, many personas, many communities, concurrent and fault-isolated. A person's trust graph stops being a single closed silo and becomes the thing they actually live in.

Build once, reuse everywhere

Issuance mirrors presentation; every ceremony is one pipeline; the vault abstracts every format. New credential types, new ceremonies, and new formats are arms on existing machinery — not new subsystems.

Future unlocks

What this foundation makes cheap to build next

Each of these was expensive-or-impossible before; each is now a small increment on machinery that already exists.

Portable, cross-community reputation

A Role VEC or endorsement earned in one community becomes evidence presentable to another. Cross-community recognition + trust-registry sync turn “member here” into “trusted there.”

Unlinkable membership & ZK predicates

BBS+ gives unlinkable presentations; the gated Circom option gives ZK predicates — prove “is a member” or “age over 18” with nothing else revealed. The vault is already format-agnostic; these slot in.

External verifiers & an open ecosystem

Because everything is W3C VC/VP over open standards, a verifier we've never met can check a member's credential. Trust stops ending at our walls.

Self-governing communities

The simulator + Rule IR + signed policy log + M-of-N governance let communities evolve their own admission, role, and removal rules safely — delegated invitation, quorum review, threaded request_more negotiation.

Air-gapped & sealed onboarding

Sealed issuance to an unknown holder (HPKE) means inviting someone who isn't online yet, or onboarding across an air gap, with no privilege escalation through the relayer.

Mobile, passkeys & step-up

A mobile authenticator that holds the persona and approves AAL1→AAL2 step-up (WebAuthn passkey) over the same DIDComm/Trust-Task surface — the hold/present agent in your pocket.

Hierarchical trust contexts

Folder-structured contexts with folder-level admin let large organisations model departments, working groups, and sub-communities under one account graph.

New ceremonies, near-free

Personhood assertions, relationship publishing, endorsement issuance — each is a policy module + evidence slot + effect handler on the pipeline that already runs join, leave, role-change, and directory.
